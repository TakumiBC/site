Divination **is** banned, but not the modern common sense of "divination". The Bible also didn't ban predicting the future.

First, we need to clarify: The Old Testament was written in Hebrew, not English. And as we all know, translations can never provide 100% accurate meaning. The differences between languages make this kind of error inevitable.

The verse that is usually cited against divination is Deuteronomy 18:10-11. We all know that the KJV is the only correct version of the Bible, and it has more authority than the original Hebrew and Greek text. Therefore, I’m going to use the NIV:

> Let no one be found among you who sacrifices their son or daughter in the fire, who practices divination or sorcery, interprets omens, engages in witchcraft,  or casts spells, or who is a medium or spiritist or who consults the dead.

And the problem is here: though divination is the best word to translate the verse, it still brings confusion, since "divination" is way broader than the original Hebrew meaning.

Cambridge Dictionary defines divination as "the skill or act of saying or discovering what will happen in the future". If we go by this definition, it is going to be disastrous. This means that all kinds of predictions of the future shall be banned, including weather forecasts, and even AI models like ChatGPT — because its principle is to predict the next token (if you don't have knowledge about AI, just think of tokens as words).

And this is why we shouldn’t rely on an English dictionary in this case. In order to interpret the Bible accurately, we need to look at the Hebrew word instead.

The original word for divination is קֶסֶם, transliterated as "qesem". The definition of qesem here is "the attempt to gain **supernatural knowledge or power** apart from the revealed will of God".

So, more precisely, the divination banned by the Bible is supernatural divination. This means that non-supernatural divination methods might be allowed.

Now we need to distinguish one thing: what exactly counts as supernatural?

No. Science isn't what the Bible forced us to align with. Things can be non-scientific while still being non-supernatural.

For example, Tzu-wei tou-shu (紫微斗數) is a popular Chinese metaphysical system. However, the process of calculating the chart can be done completely by computers, and even AIs or people with no prior knowledge can interpret it after consulting professional sources. Computers do not possess supernatural power, and most ordinary interpreters neither possess nor claim any supernatural power. Therefore, it is not inherently supernatural and not explicitly banned unless the interpreter claims that he possesses supernatural power or receives supernatural revelation.

However, this does not mean that every practice is allowed. Astrology, for example, is condemned in other biblical passages. Supernatural divination is still banned as well. For example, Tarot, séances, Ouija boards, spirit-medium practices, necromancy, and all forms of fortune-telling that claim to receive messages from spirits clearly fall into the banned category. There's no room for these practices, even after clarifying the Hebrew meaning.

Finally, as usual, some off-topic thoughts on Science and Christianity. Nowhere in the Bible had ever said "thou shalt believe in science solely" or "thou shalt believe in Western medicine solely". Jesus didn't use Western medicine at all, and he still cured people faster and better than any form of medicine humanity has ever invented.

This means that non-scientific yet non-supernatural things that you believe and that align with Christianity could and should be treated in the same way you treat science. Science can be wrong, and if you've had enough experience, there are lots of situations where science just doesn't work.
